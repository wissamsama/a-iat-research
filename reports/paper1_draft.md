## 2. Related Work [READY]

Direct draft from `paper_master_plan.md` §2's verified literature table.

> Diffusion-based reconstruction under sparse observation has been explored
> along two largely disjoint lines. The first targets tidal/coastal flood
> forecasting directly: DIFF-SPARSE (arXiv:2505.05381) diffuses a coastal
> water-level field under 0/50/95% sparsity and reports up to 62% error
> reduction over baseline methods, but does not include a parameter-matched
> deterministic control, does not diagnose a failure mechanism for its own
> parametrization, and does not evaluate the calibration of its generated
> ensembles. Spatially-Aware Diffusion (arXiv:2409.00230) reconstructs
> static PDE fields from sparse sensors with a hybrid conditioning scheme
> close to our spatial encoder, and — notably — independently concludes that
> a deterministic model wins in the noise-free regime while diffusion only
> helps once observation noise is introduced; we extend this finding to the
> *autoregressive*, real-benchmark setting and quantify a concrete
> parametrization-level mechanism for it. A second line applies conditional
> diffusion to data assimilation at national/global scale for weather and
> ocean state (km-scale station networks, JAMES 2025; ocean DA, JAMES 2025;
> PhyDA) — an active area, but none of this work runs a matched
> deterministic control, and none targets pluvial/fluvial flood forecasting.
> SF2Bench provides real river-gauge time series but no spatial field
> reconstruction, making it complementary rather than substitutable.
> FloodCastBench (Xu et al., *Scientific Data* 2025) is, to our knowledge,
> the only public benchmark combining high-fidelity flood fields, physical
> forcings (DEM, rainfall), and neural-operator baselines (U-Net, FNO,
> FNO+) at the resolution and cadence used here, and is the benchmark we
> build on.

into the Introduction's contribution list): *no existing work runs a

## 3. Problem Setup [READY]

### 3.1 Task

> We consider the task of forecasting the spatiotemporal evolution of a
> flood — a dense field of water depth over a fixed spatial grid — from a
> **partially observed** current state. At full observation density
> ("dense"), the current water-depth field is known everywhere; under
> sparsity, only a subset of grid cells (a proxy for a real sensor network)
> is observed at each time step, with the rest masked. The forecasting
> horizon is autoregressive: models roll forward multiple 300-second steps,
> re-consuming their own (or the sparsely-observed) state at each step.

### 3.2 Benchmark

> We use FloodCastBench's high-fidelity Australia event at 60m resolution
> [cite Xu et al. 2025]: 2,881 water-depth frames at 300s cadence (536×536
> grid), paired with a static DEM and time-varying rainfall forcing at
> 1800s resolution (repeated across the six 300s frames each covers,
> matching the official data-generation convention — verified against the
> `HydroPML` data-generation code, `current_time / 1800`). We report a
> second event (UK, 2015) for generalization [WP8/WP10 — see §6 Limitations
> until complete].

### 3.3 Sparsity protocol

> We evaluate three observation regimes: dense (100% observed), m50 (50%
> missing), and m95 (95% missing) — a random i.i.d. mask sampled per
> evaluation window from a fixed bank of 10 masks (seeded, reused across
> models for a fair comparison). To test whether conclusions drawn under
> random masking generalize to *realistic* sensor deployments, we
> additionally evaluate two structured mask families never seen during
> training: a **gauge** proxy (sensors weighted toward high initial
> water-occupancy cells, approximating river-gauge placement) and a
> **cluster** mask (spatially contiguous unobserved regions, approximating
> a coverage gap) — see §5.8 / F8.

### 3.4 What "calibration" can mean here — an honesty note

> FloodCastBench's ground truth is generated by a single deterministic
> hydraulic simulation (Saint-Venant) per event — there is no ensemble of
> physical realizations to calibrate against. The uncertainty a generative
> model's scenario spread can meaningfully quantify here is therefore the
> **ambiguity of reconstruction under partial observation** (how many
> distinct complete fields are consistent with a given sparse observation),
> not aleatoric physical uncertainty. We evaluate calibration under this
> reading throughout, and state it explicitly rather than let the term
> imply more than the data can support.

## 4. Methods [READY]

### 4.1 The controlled pair: V2 and its deterministic twin

> To isolate whether a generative model's value comes from its generative
> mechanism specifically — as opposed to its architecture, training budget,
> or context length — we construct a **deterministic twin**: a model
> sharing V2's exact encoders (temporal context encoder, pixel-aligned
> spatial encoder), backbone (2D UNet), parameter count (verified by direct
> comparison, not approximate), training budget, checkpoint-selection
> protocol, and pushforward scheme, with the diffusion loop replaced by a
> single deterministic forward pass (noise input fixed to zero, no
> diffusion timestep). This is, to our knowledge, the first such matched
> control run for autoregressive flood forecasting under sparsity.

> **A statistical trap in comparing the two, and why it matters.** For any
> posterior distribution, E[(sample − truth)²] = Var(posterior) +
> E[(mean − truth)²] ≥ E[(mean − truth)²]: a single draw from a generative
> model carries strictly more expected squared error than the posterior
> mean, purely as a property of variance, regardless of model quality. A
> deterministic regressor targets the mean directly. Comparing a single
> generative draw to a deterministic output on RMSE is therefore
> **mathematically biased toward the deterministic model by construction**
> — we discovered this affecting our own internal checkpoint-selection
> metric mid-project (it uses a single rollout draw) and correct for it by
> reporting only twin-vs-*mean-of-8-scenarios* comparisons as decision-
> relevant throughout the paper. We flag this explicitly because it is a
> generic trap for any future deterministic-vs-generative comparison in
> this literature, not specific to our setup.

### 4.2 Why the field's absolute value cannot be diffused directly at fine cadence

> [Motivating mechanism — precedes the delta parametrization below.] A
> first attempt (V1) diffusing the *absolute* water-depth field at the
> benchmark's native 300-second cadence fails catastrophically in the dense
> regime: ~560× worse than trivial persistence (predicting no change).  We
> trace this to a scale mismatch, not an implementation defect: the true
> physical signal between two 300-second frames (σ_Δ, the frame-to-frame
> standard deviation) is tiny relative to the field's own scale (σ_field) —
> we measure the ratio at **σ_field/σ_Δ ≈ 488 (Australia) / 425 (UK)**
> across both tested events (F2). A diffusion sampler's own residual error
> scales with the magnitude of *its generative target*; diffusing the
> absolute field makes that target the full field scale, so beating
> persistence requires a per-step sampling error under ~0.2% of the field's
> own range — below what a realistic diffusion sampler achieves at this
> resolution. **V2** instead diffuses the *delta* (the frame-to-frame
> change), for which the equivalent condition is trivially satisfied.

### 4.3 Per-regime scale under partial observation

> Under sparsity, a further complication: an observed pixel's natural
> target scale is the delta from its last known value (σ_Δ); a masked
> pixel's target is the delta from the *training-mean fill*, whose scale is
> closer to σ_field. A single global scale for both regimes either drowns
> the observed-pixel signal or destabilizes the masked-pixel one — an
> instability we hit directly during development (Incident 2026-07-09:
> NaN batches under exactly this defect, corrected and regression-tested,
> 24/24 smoke tests). V2 therefore uses a **per-pixel, regime-aware scale**,
> resolved at each pixel from its observed/masked status at the current
> step.

## 5.3-bis. V2 dense vs the *published* FNO+ number, all metrics [READY]

> A natural objection to any "V2 beats FNO+" claim is that it might simply
> reflect an imperfect FNO+ reproduction (our own reproduction sits ×1.66
> above the published Table 4 relRMSE, a gap traced to undisclosed
> preprocessing details, not a bug — see Appendix). We therefore also
> report V2 against the **published** number directly, sidestepping that
> objection entirely:

| Metric | FNO+ (Table 4, published) | V2 dense (mean±std, 3 seeds) | 
|---|---:|---:|
| relRMSE | 0.003941 | 0.001576 ± 0.000818 |
| NSE | 0.999979 | 0.999996 ± 0.000004 |
| Pearson r | 0.999990 | 0.999999 ± 0.000001 |
| CSI@0.001 | 0.939638 | 0.986660 ± 0.003735 |
| CSI@0.01 | 0.984588 | 0.999098 ± 0.000393 |

> V2 outperforms the published FNO+ result on **all five** reported
> metrics, not a single cherry-picked one. This holds even though V2's
> input context (24 frames) exceeds FNO+'s (1 frame, verified from
> `FloodCastBenchFNOPlusOfficialDataset` — the "20-frame" input tensor
> broadcasts one observed value across all positions, not a real temporal
> signal) — a natural objection we tested directly rather than dismissed:
> giving FNO+ the *same* 24-frame context (WPB0) makes it **worse**, not
> better (relRMSE 0.007529±0.000328 vs its own 0.006550±0.000135 baseline,
> confirmed 3 seeds), so extra context alone does not explain V2's margin.
> *[Caveat to resolve before freezing: current V2 numbers are a 4/13-window
> quick read, not the full test protocol — rerun at 13/13 before citing as
> final.]*

## 5.8. Structured masks vs random (F8) [READY]

> Training uses i.i.d. random observation masks; deployment sensor networks
> are not i.i.d. We test two structured mask families never seen in
> training, at matched sensor budget (§3.3):

| Mask | m50 relRMSE | m95 relRMSE | vs. random baseline |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Random (training distribution) | 0.395 | 0.567 | — |
| Gauge (spatially distributed, water-occupancy-weighted) | 0.393 | 0.585 | ≈ identical |
| Cluster (contiguous unobserved region) | 0.609 | 0.706 | **+54% / +25%** |

> The **gauge** mask — despite being structured rather than i.i.d. — remains
> spatially distributed, and the model generalizes to it almost perfectly.
> The **cluster** mask leaves a large contiguous region entirely
> unobserved, a spatial regime never encountered during training, and
> degrades markedly — confirmed on a second seed for the strongest cell
> (cluster/m50: relRMSE 0.597 vs 0.609, seed7 vs seed42). This is a real,
> physically interpretable generalization boundary: the model's implicit
> assumption is *distributed* partial observation, not merely a sensor
> *count* — worth stating plainly as a deployment limitation rather than
> smoothing over it.

## 7. Reproducibility Statement [READY, skeleton]

> Code, configs, and seeds for every reported run are versioned in git
> [repo link at freeze time]. All headline comparisons use ≥3 seeds (R1);
> no single-seed number is reported without an explicit label ruling out
> generalization. Every model comparison holds context length and
> evaluation protocol fixed, with any remaining asymmetry stated next to
> the number (R2). Every sparsity regime is smoke-tested before a training
> queue is launched (R3) — a discipline adopted after a live incident
> (Incident 2026-07-09: an unguarded scale term produced NaN batches under
> the sparse regime specifically, caught and fixed same-day, 24/24 tests
> green before resuming). A statistical bias specific to
> deterministic-vs-generative comparisons (§4.1) is documented as a
> permanent methodological rule (R9) after being caught live on this
